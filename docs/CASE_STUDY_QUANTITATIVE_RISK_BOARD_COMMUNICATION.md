# Case Study: Quantitative Risk - Translating Security Findings for Board Decisions

## 2-Minute Interview Script
"In A.V.A.R.S, I wanted to prove I could communicate cyber risk in business terms, not only technical terms. I built a quantitative risk workflow that converts incident and vulnerability scenarios into financial exposure using ALE and Monte Carlo simulation. This allowed me to present trade-offs clearly: expected annual loss, likelihood bands, and the financial impact of faster containment controls. For board-level communication, this is critical because it links security engineering decisions to budget and risk appetite."

## STAR Answer
### Situation
Technical findings were available, but decision-makers needed financially meaningful risk insights for prioritization.

### Task
Translate technical security outcomes into board-ready risk language for investment and control decisions.

### Action
- Implemented risk quantification logic in the project workflow.
- Modeled risk ranges with Monte Carlo simulation and ALE-style calculations.
- Connected control improvements (for example faster containment) to reduced expected loss.
- Produced structured reporting suitable for executive/non-technical stakeholders.

### Result
- Improved prioritization clarity for remediation and response controls.
- Enabled business-facing explanation of why detection/response improvements matter financially.
- Demonstrated Security+ risk-management competency in a measurable way.

## Situation
Security controls were improving technically, but leadership-level decision making required quantified impact, not just severity labels.

## Task
Provide credible risk quantification outputs to support control prioritization and budget rationale.

## Actions
- Used project risk scripts to estimate expected loss ranges.
- Compared baseline vs improved response scenarios.
- Mapped technical control changes to financial-risk deltas.

## Result
- Created board-facing narratives that connect SOC performance and business risk.
- Improved communication between technical and non-technical stakeholders.

## Evidence Points to Discuss in Interview
- Difference between severity and business impact.
- Why ranges and confidence intervals are more useful than single-point numbers.
- How quantitative framing supports governance and investment decisions.

## Competency Mapping
- Security+: Risk management and business impact communication.
- SC-200: Operational outcomes tied to detection and response quality.
- MSc Cybersecurity + AI: Data-driven decision support and analytical rigor.
